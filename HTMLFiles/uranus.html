U r a n u s

Surface And Atmosphere

  • There is no well-defined solid surface within Uranus's interior.
  • The outermost part of Uranus's gaseous envelope that is accessible to remote sensing is called its atmosphere.
  • The tenuous thermosphere extends over two planetary radii from the nominal surface, which is defined to lie at a pressure of 1 bar.
  • The Uranian atmosphere can be divided into three layers: the troposphere, the stratosphere and the thermosphere.
  • The composition of Uranus's atmosphere is different from its bulk, consisting mainly of molecular hydrogen and helium.
  • The abundances of less volatile compounds such as ammonia, water, and hydrogen sulfide in the deep atmosphere are poorly known.
  • The outermost layer of the Uranian atmosphere is the thermosphere and corona.

Characteristics

  • At ultraviolet and visible wavelengths, Uranus's atmosphere is bland in comparison to the other giant planets.
  • Recent observation also discovered that cloud features on Uranus have a lot in common with those on Neptune.
  • The tracking of numerous cloud features allowed determination of zonal winds blowing in the upper troposphere of Uranus.
  • There are some indications that physical seasonal changes are happening in Uranus. Although Uranus is known to have a bright south polar region, the north pole is fairly dim.
  • Uranus's internal heat appears markedly lower than that of the other giant planets; in astronomical terms, it has a low thermal flux.
  • The lowest temperature recorded in Uranus's tropopause is 49 K, making Uranus the coldest planet in the Solar System.

Neighbours

  • Uranus has 27 known natural satellites.
  • The five main satellites are Miranda, Ariel, Umbriel, Titania, and Oberon.
  • The Uranian satellite system is the least massive among those of the giant planets; the combined mass of the five major satellites would be less than half that of Triton (largest moon of Neptune).
  • The largest of Uranus's satellites, Titania, has a radius of less than half that of the Moon.
  • Among the Uranian satellites, Ariel appears to have the youngest surface, with the fewest impact craters, and Umbriel the oldest.
  • The Uranian rings are composed of extremely dark particles, which vary in size from micrometres to a fraction of a metre.
  • The matter in the rings may once have been part of a moon (or moons) that was shattered by high-speed impacts.
